    Mycelium youth network is a non profit based on unceded Ohlone land.

    Our garden project sits on Ramaytush Ohlone land. The Ramaytush are the native peoples of the San Francisco Peninsula. Most descendants of the indigenous groups of the San Francisco Bay Area, however, refer to themselves as Ohlone while a few others use Costanoan. Prior to the arrival of the Spanish in 1769, the Ramaytush Ohlone numbered approximately 1500 persons and lived in ten small tribal groups. All the projects enacted on Ohlone land by Mycelium have the goal of some way benefiting the Ohlone land and people through environmental means.


    Anne Marie Sayers Leader

    Ann Marie Sayers is an Ohlone tribal leader, environmental activist, and the tribal chair of Indian Canyon.

    Who is Anne Marie Sayers?

    Anne Marie Sayers is a respected elder and tribal chairwoman of the Indian Canyon Nation, a group recognized by the State of California as comprising descendants of the Verona Band of Alameda County Ohlone. She is a prominent figure in the Native American community, particularly in the San Francisco Bay Area. Sayers has been deeply involved in indigenous activism, cultural preservation, and land rights advocacy for decades. She has worked tirelessly to raise awareness about the history, culture, and struggles of the Ohlone people, including their connection to the land and their ongoing efforts to reclaim their ancestral territories.


    Ramaytush Ohlone land projects

    Traditional Land Stewardship: Ramaytush Ohlone land projects may involve the restoration and conservation of traditional lands using Indigenous land management practices. This can include activities such as controlled burning to manage vegetation, restoring native plant species, and implementing sustainable land-use practices that enhance ecosystem health and resilience to climate change impacts.




    Carbon Sequestration: By restoring native vegetation and implementing sustainable land management practices, Ramaytush Ohlone land projects can contribute to carbon sequestration. Healthy ecosystems, including forests, wetlands, and grasslands, act as carbon sinks, absorbing carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and helping to mitigate climate change.




    Cultural Revitalization and Education: Ramaytush Ohlone land projects provide opportunities for cultural revitalization and education within the community and beyond. By reconnecting with their ancestral lands and traditional knowledge systems, Ramaytush Ohlone people can strengthen their cultural identity and pass on valuable ecological knowledge to future generations. Educational programs and partnerships with schools and community organizations can also raise awareness about Indigenous perspectives on land stewardship and climate change.
